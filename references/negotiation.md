# Negotiation Reference — Enterprise SaaS

## Negotiation Principles

- **Never negotiate against yourself** — every concession must come in exchange for something of value
- **Understand position vs. interest** — "30% discount" is a position; "show CFO we got a good deal" is the interest
- **The first number sets the range** — anchor at or above list with justification
- **Silence is a tactic** — after stating your position, stop talking
- **Slow down to speed up** — deliberate pace signals confidence

---

## The Concession Framework (3 Tiers)

| Tier | What you give | What you get in return |
|------|--------------|----------------------|
| Tier 1 (easy) | Payment terms, PS credits, onboarding support, SLA uplifts | Faster signature, reference rights, multi-year term |
| Tier 2 (moderate) | First-year discount, additional seats, implementation support | 2–3 year commit, auto-renewal, expansion commitment |
| Tier 3 (significant) | Meaningful price reduction | Multi-year commit at full scope, executive reference, strategic partnership |

**Rules:** Never jump to Tier 3. Concede in decreasing increments. Always ask for something in return first.

## Common Procurement Tactics & Counters

**"Your competitor quoted us 40% less."**
> "I'd want to make sure we're comparing apples to apples — can you share what's in their proposal? The biggest differences are usually in implementation, support tier, or included features. Let's make sure you're not solving for price today and paying for it in year two."

**"We need a 25% discount to get this through procurement."**
> "Help me understand what's behind that number — is this a budget constraint, a procurement policy, or a competitive situation? If I can get to a number you can take to your CFO, what would you need to give me in return? I'm thinking [multi-year / faster close / expanded scope]."

**"We'll only sign a 1-year contract."**
> "Can you help me understand what's behind that? If it's about proving value, we could structure year 1 as a smaller scope with a committed expansion in year 2. Would that work?"

**"We're very close to going with [Competitor]."**
Don't panic. Don't give a major concession immediately.
> "Can you tell me what's driving that — is it purely price, or are there capability gaps? I want to make sure we're losing on the right thing before accepting that."

**Last-minute price ask (after verbal agreement):**
> "We agreed on [terms] — I've already gone to bat internally to get you that deal. If I go back now, it reopens everything and could push your timeline. I want to protect the deal we have. Can we move forward on the terms we agreed?"

## Protecting Price

Before touching ACV:
1. **Add value** — implementation support, training credits, additional seats
2. **Restructure** — ramp pricing, payment terms, delayed billing start
3. **Scope adjustment** — reduce scope to hit budget, with expansion path
4. **Multi-year in exchange** — "I can lock in year 1 pricing for all 3 years if you commit to a 3-year term"
5. **Only then, price** — with business justification for deal desk

---

## Walk-Away Scenarios

Walk away if:
- Discount required exceeds deal desk authority AND EB won't engage to explain strategic value
- Paper process terms would expose your company to unlimited liability or data ownership transfer
- Customer is using you as a negotiating lever with no real intent to switch
- Deal economics are negative after all concessions

**Walking away correctly:**
> "I've worked hard to find a path here, and I don't think I can get to a place that works for both of us. I'd rather be honest about that now than waste more of your time. If your situation changes, I hope you'll come back to us."

A clean walk-away often re-opens deals 30–90 days later.
